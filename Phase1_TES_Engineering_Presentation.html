Executive Summary

Objective: Evaluate Thermal Energy Storage (TES) as replacement for natural gas backup to achieve 24/7 carbon-free energy for off-grid datacenter operations.

Key Findings

  • Technically viable: TES provides long-duration backup at 100 MW scale using commercially proven firebrick storage (1,500°C)
  • Cost competitive: $40/kWh storage cost - lowest among long-duration energy storage (LDES) technologies
  • Parameters validated: Steam turbine efficiency 34-39% confirmed (NREL SAM, EPA CHP Catalog, MIT)
  • Critical constraint: Turbine must operate ≥40% thermal capacity (physics: steam flow instability below 40%)

System Architecture: Two-Mode Operation

⚡ CHARGING MODE (Daytime - Excess Solar)

Flow: Solar → Electric Heater (95% efficient) → Thermal Storage (1,500°C firebrick)

  • Charging power: 300 MW electric input
  • Duration: ~6 hours peak solar (10 AM - 4 PM)
  • Daily capacity: 1,600 MWh thermal stored
🔥 DISCHARGING MODE (Nighttime - Renewable Gap)

Flow: Thermal Storage → Steam Turbine (34-39% efficient) → Electric Output → Datacenter

  • Discharge power: 100 MW thermal capacity
  • Electric output: 34-39 MW (depending on load: 40-100%)
  • Duration: 16 hours at full discharge
Design Rationale: 3:1 charge/discharge ratio (300 MW / 100 MW) enables rapid recharge during limited solar window while providing extended overnight backup.

Engineering Constraints & Design Limitations

Constraint Specification Implication
Minimum Load (40%) ≥15.6 MW Turbine must shut down if below 40% thermal capacity. No flexible partial backup.
Ramp Rate 10-15 MW/min Slower than gas turbines (30-50 MW/min). Requires advance scheduling.
Cold Start Time 30-60 minutes No immediate backup power. Must be pre-warmed or paired with battery.
Hot Start Time 10-15 minutes If recently shut down (within 8 hours), restart is faster.
Thermal Losses ~1% per day Best for daily cycling, not seasonal storage.
Water Requirement ~1,000 gal/MWh Steam turbine requires water for condensing cycle.

Key Design Trade-Offs

  • Low RTE (32-33%): Requires 3x renewable overbuild vs. batteries. BUT: Low $/kWh ($40 vs. $150) makes this favorable for long-duration.
  • Minimum load constraint: Limits flexibility. For constant 100 MW datacenter load, this is manageable.
  • Cold start time: TES cannot replace spinning reserve. Best for predictable, scheduled discharge (overnight backup).

  • Operational Experience: Thousands of units deployed globally. Well-understood maintenance, reliability, performance.

Mitigation: Pilot 100-200 MWh system first to validate performance before full-scale deployment.
