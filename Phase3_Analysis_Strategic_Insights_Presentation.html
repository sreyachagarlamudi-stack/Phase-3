Phase 3 provides comprehensive analysis to support strategic decision-making on achieving 24/7 carbon-free energy for datacenter operations.

FOUR CORE QUESTIONS ANALYZED WITH PHASE 2 OPTIMIZATION MODEL

Key Questions

1. What is the optimal mix of wind, solar, and TES to achieve 100% CFE at lowest LCOE?
2. How does TES compare to alternative LDES technologies (Li-ion, flow batteries, hydrogen)?
3. What is the impact of policy levers (IRA, carbon pricing, grid interconnection, RECs)?
4. What is the strategic path to eliminate natural gas completely?

Methodology

  • Optimization approach: Rolling 48-hour windows with tuning inputs to incentivize energy banking - more efficient and realistic than full-year MIP
  • Optimization scenarios: 20+ combinations of solar (300-500 MW), wind (50-200 MW), TES (150-200 MW)
  • Technology comparison: Capital costs, round-trip efficiency, and LCOE for TES, Li-ion, flow batteries, hydrogen
  • Policy sensitivity: IRA 30% ITC, carbon pricing ($0-300/ton), 24/7 CFE RECs
  • Phased deployment: 90% CFE intermediate target to reduce capital requirements and risk

Answer 2: TES vs. Alternative LDES Technologies

For 44-hour, 180 MW system required to achieve 100% CFE, TES has lowest capital cost despite lower round-trip efficiency.

TES COST ADVANTAGE: $40/kWh vs $150-250/kWh for batteries (3-6X cheaper per unit storage)
Technology Net CapEx Cost vs. TES RTE Key Trade-Off
TES (Thermal) $523M Baseline 33% Lowest $/kWh ($40), but requires 3x renewable overbuild
Hydrogen (H₂) $611M +17% 38% Similar RTE to TES, but electrolyzer/fuel cell expensive. Best for seasonal.
Lithium-Ion $1,028M +97% 88% High RTE, but $/kWh ($150-180) makes 44-hour duration prohibitively expensive.
Flow Battery $1,258M +141% 70% Decoupled energy/power, but high $/kWh ($200-250) and pre-commercial (TRL 7-8).

Why TES Wins for 24/7 Datacenter CFE

  • Long-duration cost advantage: $40/kWh beats all alternatives for 44-hour duration. Li-ion and flow batteries economically viable only for 2-4 hours.
  • Renewable overbuild trade-off: TES's low RTE (33%) requires 400 MW solar + 125 MW wind to serve 100 MW load. However, renewable CapEx declining faster than battery costs.
  • Commercial maturity: TES TRL 8-9 (Rondo, Anora deployments) vs. flow batteries TRL 7-8 (pre-commercial) reduces deployment risk.
  • No geographic constraints: Unlike pumped hydro (requires elevation + water) or CAES (requires salt caverns), TES deployable anywhere.

Answer 3B: Policy Impact - Carbon Pricing

Carbon pricing changes relative economics of gas backup vs. TES storage by adding cost to fossil fuel emissions.

CARBON PRICE CROSSOVER AT $200/TON: TES AND GAS BECOME ECONOMICALLY EQUIVALENT

Carbon Pricing Scenarios

Carbon Price Gas Cost (Base + Carbon) TES Equivalent Cost Economic Outcome
$0/ton (current) $40/MWh $125/MWh Gas much cheaper - requires gas limit constraint to achieve 100% CFE
$100/ton $80/MWh $125/MWh Gap narrows, but gas still cheaper
$200/ton $120/MWh $125/MWh Crossover point - gas and TES economically equivalent
$300/ton $160/MWh $125/MWh TES cheaper than gas - 100% CFE achievable without explicit gas limit

Natural Gas Carbon Intensity

Emissions: 0.4 ton CO₂/MWh (EPA factor for natural gas combined-cycle plants)
Effective cost with carbon price: Base cost + (Carbon price × Emissions)
Example at $200/ton: $40/MWh base + ($200/ton × 0.4 ton/MWh) = $120/MWh effective cost

Policy Outlook

  • Current status: No federal carbon price in U.S. (as of 2026). Some states (CA, RGGI) have regional carbon markets.
  • Trajectory: Biden administration proposed $25-50/ton carbon price, increasing to $100-150/ton by 2030.
  • Impact on 100% CFE: At $100-200/ton carbon price, TES economics become competitive with gas backup even without IRA credits.
